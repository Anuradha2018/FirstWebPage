ABOUT US

Welcome to the Bioanalytics research group!

The Bioanalytics group employs micro- and nanotechnology, microfluidics and multi-parameter detection systems (both optical and electrochemical) for the development and characterization of integrated devices with applications in tissue engineering, drug delivery and monitoring biological processes at cellular level. With the use of 3D printing and other modern micro- and nanofabrication technologies, biomimetic membranes and 3D carbon scaffolds with promising applications in biology and medicine are being designed and tested as part of the interdisciplinary tasks that this group set out to accomplish.

Keywords:
  • Bioanalytics, Bioelectrochemistry & Bioimpedance
  • Biosensors & LOC-developments
  • 3D Printing & Bioprinting
  • 3D Conductive Materials & Scaffolds
  • Biomimetics: Cell, Tissue & Organ Models

RESEARCH

Bioimpedance based Lab-on-a-Chip (LOC) systems using 3D printing

Recently rapid prototyping technology and in particular 3D printing technologies are increasingly being exploited for tissue engineering applications. The main challenge in tissue engineering organs is that the engineered organ must mimic the physiological function of the biological organ. 3D printing technology allows us to tailor the microarchitecture of scaffolds and particularly enables the possibility of developing high resolution channels to supply nutrients to cells. Additionally, this technology can be used to fabricate intelligent biochips and microfluidic systems incorporating scaffolds embedded with cells and electrodes for monitoring growth and proliferation of cells. The goal of this project is to optimize 3D printing technology for production of system components for a bioartificial liver on a chip system. This includes development of biochips, tissue engineering scaffolds and microfluidic system components. This project will be carried on in synergy with the EU funded NanoBio4Trans project whose overall aim is to engineer a highly vascularized artificial liver using a combination of stem cell, 3D scaffold and micro-nano sensing technologies.

Conducting pyrolysed 3D carbon scaffolds for cell replacement therapy

This project involves biological exploration of pyrolysed 3D carbon scaffold (p3D-CS) materials of different topography, porosity and conductivity. The biological background is stem cell research for Parkinson´s disease for which we in a recent study have shown that an SU8 derived p3D-CS (Fig 2) remarkably boosted the differentiation of human neuronal stem cells (hNSCs) into dopaminergic neurons (dopamine producing neurons), and that the scaffold moreover could be used to directly sense and confirm the dopaminergic phenotype of these neurons (Fig. 2). This PhD project intends to: (1) set up an analytical framework that will aid in obtaining a better understanding of the underlying mechanism for such previously unseen differentiation effects on any material, and (2) find new carbon materials and structures that can be explored for cell replacement therapy (CRT) and tissue engineering applications for the future.

Real-time monitoring of stem cell differentiation in brain tissue using microfluidics

Since the early 1960’s the treatment of PD has been based on medication supplying L-DOPA, an intermediate of DA synthesis in functional cells, to alleviate the symptoms of the impaired motor function. Although this treatment has shown positive effects, it only relieves the symptoms in the early stage of PD without providing a long-term cure. Translantation of dopaminergic neuronal stem cells (NSCs) (stem cell therapy) has become a new possible long-term treatment. NSCs can integrate into the striatum, replenishing the supply of dopamine, has been proposed as an alternative [2, 3]. In this project stem cell therapy for the treatment of Parkinson’s disease is under focus. The main goal is to establish a microfluidic online culture system with integrated electrodes to characterize and optimize the differentiation of NSCs so that they function in the same manner as native dopaminergic neurons. Furthermore the synaptic connections formed between the differentiated NSCs and the primary neurons from striatum will be investigated. In order to study the formation of synaptic connections between native neurons and neuronal stem cells, there is a need for the establishment of a microfluidic platform with integrated electrode chips where primary neurons from striatum can be co-cultured with NSCs. This type of system can facilitate specific stimulation of neuronal stem cells and signal recording from the co-cultured primary neurons. For the establishment of this experimental approach, there is the need to optimize transfection of neuronal stem cells to enable optogenetic modification that allows stimulation of the cells by using a specific wavelength of light. Fluorescence based detections will be used to monitor influx of calcium in the neuronal stem cells during exocytosis event and molecular beacon will be used to visualize mRNA appearance when tyrosine Hydroxylase is synthesized.

OUR TEAM

Professor Jenny Emneus

Jenny Emnéus pursued her academic career at Lund University, Sweden and was recruited as a Professor at DTU Nanotech in 2007 where she leads the LOC strategic field and Bioanalytics group. She has coordinated 7 EU projects and currently has two Marie Curie ITN projects in H2020, one of which she is the coordinator (Training4CRM. She has served as an evaluator and expert panel member of e.g. Swedish Research Council, EU FP7 projects for the environment program and is since 2012 a member of the LS9 panel for ERC starting grant. She has vast experience of development and application of biosensors and 2D and 3D LOC devices. Her main research interest is focused on microfluidic bioassay systems (enzymes, antibodies and cells) using multi-parameter detection systems (optical and electrochemical), and organ-on-a chip system and 3D scaffolds tissue engineering.
